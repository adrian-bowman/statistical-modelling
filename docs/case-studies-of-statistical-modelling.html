16.1 Covid-19 initial surveillance

In January 2020, as concern about a new virus appearing in China was growing, the COVID-19 Response Team set up at Imperial College London published a report whose opening paragraphs are given below.

On the 31st December 2019, the World Health Organization (WHO) China Country Office was informed of cases of pneumonia of unknown aetiology in Wuhan City, Hubei Province, China. A novel Coronavirus (2019-nCoV) related to the Middle Eastern Respiratory Syndrome virus and the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome virus has since been implicated.

As of 16th January 2020, 41 cases (including two deaths) have been confirmed in Wuhan City with three confirmed cases in travellers detected in Thailand (2 cases) and Japan (1 case).

Is it possible to use the number of cases detected outside China at this stage, to estimate the number of clinically comparable cases within Wuhan City that may have occurred thus far?

16.2 UK parliamentary election 2024

The years from 2010 to 2024 was a very turbulent period in politics in the UK. It including two referenda: one on Scottish independence and another on Brexit. There were also five general elections held, in 2010, 2015, 2017, 2019 and 2024. Detailed information on the results of these general elections is available from the House of Commons Library and the datasets can be accessed through the rp.datalink function with the dataset name UK_election_2010 for the 2010 election and with corresponding names for the other years.

There are several interesting questions which we might use these data to explore.

  • What is the spatial pattern of ‘seats’ (constituencies) won by the different parties and how does this change over the period?
  • To what extent is the changing pattern of seats driven by a simple ‘swing’ in votes between the parties?

In view of the special politics in the four nations within the UK, namely England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, with some parties operating only within one of these nations, it may be helpful to separate any analysis out by nation.

A further complication is that the boundaries of some constituencies have changed over time. Geographical boundary information is available for all elections except 2010.

The boundaries for 2024 are available through rp.datalink('UK_parliamentary_boundaries_2024'). The 2024 election results should be downloaded from https://researchbriefings.files.parliament.uk/documents/CBP-10009/MPs-elected.xlsx. The path to the downloaded file is assumed to have been assigned to path.results.

A possible analysis

library(tidyverse)
library(sf)
library(readxl)
library(rpanel)

path <- rp.datalink("UK_parliamentary_boundaries_2024")
dest <- tempfile()
unzip(path, exdir = dest)
boundaries_2024 <- read_sf(dest) %>%
   rename(Constituency = PCON24NM, Code = PCON24CD)

elected_2024 <- read_excel(path.results)

# Join the shape and data information
anti_join(boundaries_2024, elected_2024, c("Code" = "ons_id"))
